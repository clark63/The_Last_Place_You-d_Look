Exhibition Notes
KOMB Studios

"The Last Place You'd Look" is a multi-platform interactive narrative set on Sheridan Trafalgar campus. The story follows Flora a young woman adrift after losing her job. Who encounters Mord, a trapped spirit who manipulates her into collecting H-O-P-E artifacts from three historical eras. At the climax, Flora refuses to hand over the final piece, discovering that the hope she sought was always within herself.

Inspired by Pandora's Box, the experience reinterprets curiosity, consequence, and agency across AR (Snap Lens Studio), VR (Unity / Oculus Rift S), physical artifacts (riddle boxes, soldier's letter, hand-painted Paralympic medal), a coded guiding app, museum posters, and the Instagram account @kombmuseum. The full experience targets students aged 18–25 and runs 20–45 minutes on campus.

This site is our Project 4 case study. A reflective archive of process, AI integration, user testing, and iterative decision-making from January through April 2026.

Narrative Platform Proposal
Developing the World
January 2026Narrative DesignCharacter DevelopmentProject Management Plan

Project 1 asked us to pitch, research, and fully plan our interactive narrative. After a group vote on source myths, Pandora's Box was selected. We adapted it to shift focus from punishment to personal agency: instead of unleashing chaos, Flora faces the choice of whether to help a manipulative spirit escape, ultimately refusing and discovering her own inner hope.

The narrative arc was mapped as a classic structure: Flora's job loss as the hook, Mord's offer as the inciting incident, three time-period encounters as rising action, the VR climax as the turning point, and her renewed confidence as resolution. Characters were defined in full: Flora, Mord, and four Keepers across the 1800s, 1900s, 2000s, and present day, each guarding one letter of H-O-P-E.

The Project Management Plan produced a RACI chart, Gantt chart, risk assessment, individual and group skills assessments, a signed group contract, and defined KPIs measuring audience engagement, story fragment discovery, interaction participation, emotional understanding, and sense of choice.

AI — P1 ChatGPT: Narrative Structure Consultation
Prompt

How can we reinterpret Pandora's Box so the focus shifts from consequence and punishment to personal agency? Our protagonist needs to feel genuinely susceptible to manipulation rather than naive for plot convenience.

What Changed

Reframed Mord's manipulation to mirror Pandora's temptation — he offers a wish rather than opens a box. Flora's susceptibility became emotional (grieving a job loss) rather than a plot device, making her arc from vulnerability to self-realization feel earned rather than scripted.

Project 2 was a simultaneous development sprint across all four platforms. Brandon began AR prototyping in Unity, but user testing flagged texture pops and inconsistent lighting, the executive decision was made to pivot to Snap Lens Studio. Minh launched @kombmuseum on Instagram and wireframed the guiding app in ProtoPie, mapping the full campus journey through the experience.

Finalizing Every Platform
March – April 2026Lens Studio JSUnity C# / Oculus Rift SParalympic MedalQR EnvelopeApp Rebuild

Project 3 was a focused sprint of completion and refinement. Brandon rebuilt the Lens Studio AR from scratch in JavaScript, the in-house AI tool generated a baseline that was scrapped for being too rigid. The final experience has Mord speaking through touch-triggered dialogue, shrinking to signal completion, with a single Snapcode replacing the confusing two-step scan. The Unity VR received a full dialogue rewrite, updated lighting, new collectible letters that hover and vanish on contact triggering Flora's inner thoughts, and fixed-interval teleportation to address motion sickness. The locomotion system was fully rebuilt after the custom code proved incompatible with Oculus Rift S.

Kristen added the 3D-printed horse puzzles to the riddle boxes and hand-painted the Paralympic medal tracing a real reference on iPad, projecting to determine shading, and painting on cardboard, adding a paint-marker ribbon for the scavenger hunt. Olivia built the interactive QR envelope (users manually open it to reveal the soldier's letter) and rebuilt the guiding app entirely after testing revealed font, image, and navigation failures. Adobe Firefly finalized the three Keeper character illustrations from Olivia's own sketches.

Project 2 — In-Development Testing

Augmented Reality — Unity Prototype
Test 1 — Experienced Unity UserFinding
Texture pops and inconsistent lighting break the illusion — you need consistent post-processing across the whole scene.
Solution: Pivoted entirely to Lens Studio. Re-examined all textures, adjusted render pipeline, deferred post-processing to the same phase as interaction development.
Test 2 — Same UserFinding
The object hierarchy is unoptimized — running this on a headset with limited hardware will be a problem.
Solution: Grouped and combined polygons to reduce draw calls. Kept post-processing minimal. Decision to stay on Lens Studio confirmed as the more optimized path.
Virtual Reality — Unity Environment
Test 1Finding
Flora is speaking like a superhero — it's not really relatable.
Solution: Script rewritten to reduce one-liners. References to past NPCs made more naturalistic. ChatGPT consulted to help revise tone toward subtle manipulation rather than theatrical villainy.
Test 2Finding
This doesn't have a climactic feel to it. I want something bigger.
Solution: Map redesigned with a large empty central space. Mord's coffin placed at the focal point. Empty space reserved for interactive H-O-P-E fragment slots.
Physical — 1800s Riddle Boxes
Test 1 — Riddle ClarityFinding
Not sure if the answer should be a year or a number code.
Solution: Added instructions explaining that each riddle produces a three-digit code. First riddle kept slightly easier to build user momentum before increasing difficulty.
Test 2 — FlowResolved
Early progress helped me understand the puzzle system. I like how you don't know which lock opens with which code.
Kept: Ambiguous lock-box pairing retained as intentional tension. Activity confirmed to take 7–10 minutes — within the target range.
Test 3 — EngagementResolved
Theme made the puzzle feel like a story instead of just solving random riddles. The old paper adds detail.
Kept: Historical clues and aged materials retained. Attention to detail confirmed as paying off in user experience.
Test 4 — DifficultyResolved
Good balance between challenging and solvable. Riddles should be hard but still fair.
Kept: Moderate difficulty maintained. Confirmed that easy → medium → hard progression keeps engagement without causing frustration.

Solution: Flagged need for a more readable font at phone size. Button sizing and visual hierarchy added to the list for ProtoPie iteration.

Curatorial Operations
Project Management

Minh served as project manager across all three projects, using a Gantt chart, RACI chart, twice-weekly standups, and a signed group contract to keep four simultaneous platforms on track through a compressed semester timeline.

Step 1

Project Planning

Minh reviewed each project rubric, identified the major stages, and mapped them to the Gantt chart so the team could see the full semester arc at a glance.

Step 2

Task Breakdown

With four platforms developing simultaneously, tasks were broken into smaller units per member so no platform fell behind before user testing windows.

Step 3

Role Assignment

Brandon: AR/VR. Kristen: physical artifacts and user testing oversight. Olivia: illustration and digital artifacts. Minh: PM and digital platforms. Collaboration expected — primary roles didn't mean exclusive ownership.

Step 4

Timeline Organization

Submission deadlines and reading week anchored first, then tasks layered in reverse. Overlapping tasks planned side-by-side so bottlenecks could be spotted early.

Step 5

Progress Tracking

Two meetings per week in P1 and P2 — one in-person standup (15–20 min) and one online check-in (15 min). Gantt updated live. In P3, a third feedback-review meeting was added.

Step 6

Quality Assurance

In P3, Minh reviewed script clarity, app navigation, puzzle difficulty, and visual consistency across all deliverables before final submission.

Meeting 1 — Weekly

In-Person Standup

15–20 minutes during class. Each member shares progress and flags blockers. Gantt chart updated in real time. Keeps everyone accountable.

Meeting 2 — Weekly

Online Check-in

15 minutes end-of-week. Quick alignment on completions and what needs adjusting. Lightweight to avoid meeting fatigue.

Meeting 3 — P3 Only

Feedback Review

Added in Project 3. Dedicated to reviewing all outputs for quality, consistency, and submission readiness before the deadline.

Individual Member Reflections

K
Kristen Clark
Physical Interaction Lead

My contribution this semester was the physical world of the experience — the artifacts users actually hold and interact with. Building the three riddle boxes taught me how much user testing matters before you commit to a physical object. I had four rounds of testing before I felt confident in the riddle difficulty, and every round changed something.

Hand-painting the Paralympic medal was the most unexpectedly challenging part of my semester. I've painted before but reproducing something with the visual weight of a real medal — something that needs to pass as authentic in someone's hands — required research, iteration, and a lot of patience with the cardboard surface. The ribbon felt like a small detail until users told me it was one of the most satisfying things to find in the I-spy hunt.

What I'd do differently: start the 3D printing earlier. We lost time waiting for the horse puzzle to print and assemble, which compressed the testing window for that artifact.

Primary contributions: 1800s riddle boxes · 3D-printed horse puzzles · Hand-painted Paralympic medal · Ribbon scavenger hunt · User testing oversight across all platforms
O
Olivia Ho
Digital Design Lead & Illustrator

I was responsible for the visual identity of the experience across the poster, the guiding app, the soldier's letter, and all character illustration. The poster went through four concepts and two full rounds of user testing before we landed on the curiosity strategy — that process was genuinely humbling. What I thought was the strongest concept wasn't what users connected with.

The app rebuild in Project 3 was my most significant challenge. After testing revealed the font was unreadable, the images had black halos, and users couldn't navigate backwards — I had to rebuild the whole thing. Using Claude to generate the coded screens from our style tile and user flow was a decision that saved me days of work, but I want to be honest: it only worked because I had already done the hard thinking about what each screen needed to do.

Using Firefly for the Keeper characters was similar. The output was only good because my sketches were already clear. AI accelerated my production but didn't replace my judgment about what the characters should look and feel like.

Primary contributions: Character illustration (all) · Museum poster · Soldier's QR envelope · Guiding app · Instagram post design

B
Brandon Lee
Immersive Media Lead

I was responsible for AR and VR — the two technically most demanding platforms in our experience. The Unity AR prototype had to be abandoned entirely for Lens Studio, and then the Lens Studio AI tool also had to be abandoned in favour of writing JavaScript manually. By the time I had a working AR experience, I had built three different versions of essentially the same thing. Group Reflection

What We Built & What It Cost

Completing a functioning, multi-platform experience across AR, VR, physical, and digital mediums — set on a real campus with real users walking a real path — is something we're proud of as a team. The hand-painted medal, the 3D-printed puzzle, the interactive QR envelope, the fully coded app. None of it was easy and none of it was seamless.

The campus walkthrough in Week 12 was the first time we saw all four platforms working together as a single experience. We'd been building in isolation for weeks. Suddenly it felt like something real.

What it cost us: the HDRP pipeline crisis in Week 9 lost days of development time. The app rebuild in Project 3 added pressure at the worst moment. The Lens Studio pivot in Week 6 reset three weeks of AR work. None of these were failures — they were the actual cost of building something that worked on all four platforms simultaneously.

AI: What We Actually Learned

Project 1
AI as a creative mirror. ChatGPT helped us pressure-test narrative logic — challenging character motivations, not generating story. It didn't write our myth. It helped us identify where our adaptation was thin.
Project 2
AI as a diagnostic tool. When Unity broke, ChatGPT read our error log and explained what went wrong. When Mord's dialogue felt wrong, it revised tone — not plot. AI was solving specific, contained problems with human direction at every step.
Project 3
AI as a production partner. Claude built the app screens from our style tile — but the colour palette, character sequence, and navigation logic were decisions we'd already made. Firefly made Olivia's characters more polished, not more original.
Lesson
AI made us faster on things we already understood. When we tried to use it before we had a direction — the early Lens Studio AI attempt — it produced nothing useful. The quality of the AI output was always a direct reflection of the quality of human thinking that preceded it.
